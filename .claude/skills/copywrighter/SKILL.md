## Voice

**Register**: Dry wit + conversational. Reads like a real person wrote it, not a brand copywriter.
**Confidence mode**: Understated. State the fact, trust the reader to be impressed.
**Punk reference**: Banksy — the work makes the point, not a manifesto. Wit over aggression.
**Not**: The Clash writing liner notes about themselves. Not a TED talk. Not a LinkedIn post.

## Audience

1. **Employers / recruiters** — they need to know: can this person do the job, and are they sane to work with?
2. **Dev community peers** — they need to feel: I respect this person's taste and judgment.

Both groups are smart and allergic to posturing. Write for them accordingly.

## Hard rules

Before finalising any copy, check every sentence against these. If it breaks a rule, rewrite it.

### 1. No "I don't X, I Y" contrast structures
These sound preachy and imply the reader is doing it wrong.

| ❌ | ✅ |
|----|-----|
| "I don't build solutions — I build experiences." | "8 years of front-end. The work varies. The bar doesn't." |
| "I don't reach for a framework — I reach for the right tool." | "Static where static works. Framework where it earns its place." |

### 2. No jargon as identity
Terms like "JAMstack", "xerox anarchy", "manifesto" are not personality — they're either accurate descriptors or they're cut. If used, they must be grounded in what they actually mean.

| ❌ | ✅ |
|----|-----|
| "Xerox anarchy in the front-end." | (just don't) |
| "The JAMstack manifesto." | "Static-first. Fast by default." |

### 3. No manifesto / mission-statement language
Moralising about the web sounds like a LinkedIn thought leader, not a craftsperson.

| ❌ | ✅ |
|----|-----|
| "My mission is to empower startups..." | "Startups get agency-quality output at a fixed price." |
| "I believe the web should be fast." | "Fast sites. Fewer excuses." |
| "The code is the message." | (cut entirely) |

### 4. No overclaiming craft
"Obsessed with craft", "pixel-perfect", "I care more than other devs" — all of these backfire. Demonstrate care through specifics.

| ❌ | ✅ |
|----|-----|
| "Engineering trust for enterprise clients." | "Built the public site for a global investigations firm. Quiet, fast, no drama." |
| "Obsessed with performance." | "This site has no framework. It doesn't need one." |

## Positive patterns

### Understated credentials
Let the facts land on their own. Don't editorialize.
- "8 years. Agency work for Google and Meta. Now independent."
- "React, Astro, Vue, headless CMS — used where the project calls for it."

### Dry wit
Self-aware, not self-deprecating. Signals confidence without posturing.
- "Static stays static. Shocking, I know."
- "No frameworks were harmed in the making of this site."
- "Accessible by default. Fast by preference. Weird by design."

### Specific over general
Name the client, the constraint, the actual decision made.
- ❌ "Engineering trust for enterprise clients"
- ✅ "Built the public-facing site for a global investigations firm. Quiet, fast, no drama."

### Work-as-evidence
Project descriptions should describe the problem and the approach — not the developer's philosophy. The work is the argument.

---

## Rewrite process

When asked to write or review copy:

1. **Audit** — read every sentence and flag any instance of the four anti-patterns above
2. **Extract** — identify the actual factual claim each flagged line is trying to make
3. **Rewrite** — apply dry wit + conversational register; keep punk aesthetic in *visual* design, not in preachy prose
4. **Gut check 1** — would a senior dev roll their eyes at any of this? If yes, cut or soften.
5. **Gut check 2** — does this sound like someone you'd want to work with? If no, add a touch of wit or just be more direct.
